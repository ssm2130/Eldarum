The World of Eldarum

Cosmography, seas, and the slow grind of empires.

Eldarum is not a tidy kingdom under a single crown; it is a quilt of climates stitched together by trade winds, scripture, and fear of what sleeps inside distance. Cartographers speak of three great bodies of water—the Greywake Sea that separates Stammont from Zrelia, the Mirror Reach where merchant argosies paint wakes like silver chalk, and the Crushing Deeps beyond mapped soundings where hulls vanish without thunder. Between these waters lie peninsulas ribbed with stone, forests that remember languages no temple teaches, and mountain chains catalogued by shepherds as “the Giants’ Furniture.”

This article treats Eldarum at the scale of rumor and ledger alike: what a prince believes about his borders, what a harbor pilot believes about tides, and what a mendicant believes about omens are all political facts. Where histories contradict, both are preserved with their sponsors named when known.

Physical overview

The Northwake littoral—where Stammont’s crownlands face Zrelia across storm-scoured straits—is tempered by oceanic currents that bring mild summers and winters damp enough to rot boots. Inland, the Highfold Range lifts weather into sleet and fog; eastern grain belts drink meltwater rivers that bargemen call “the Crown’s Blood,” for taxes ride them down to Collimar as surely as barley. Southern scrublands bake under a sky so vast that temples paint blue ceilings to remind petitioners how small their grievances are.

Volcanism stitches the western arc: minor cones smoke like pipe bowls along fishing towns, while larger stratovolcanoes—among them Zehas—shape winds and harvests alike. Ash seasons fertilize vineyards; they also bury towns when the gods disagree.

Civilizations and continuity

Eldarum’s cultures inherit fragments from an Age of Titans, when beings of continent-scale appetite shaped geography like dough and left genius loci—spirits of place—as caretakers when their attention wandered. Later waves of mortal kings negotiated with those spirits or warred upon them; modern roads sometimes follow compacts older than currency. Ruins are not merely picturesque; they are receipts of bargains kept or broken.

Empire, in Eldarum, is a seasonal word. Stammont speaks of eternal crowns; Zrelian chronicles prefer cyclical renewal. City charters borrow language from both, making lawyers wealthy and historians skeptical. Frontier regions such as Asceveron exploit these ambiguities—tax collectors arrive late, prophets arrive early, and adventurers arrive with shovels.

Travel and peril

Roads belong to kings; rivers belong to bargemen; forests belong to whoever survives them. Night travel is discouraged where dream-winds blow from Titan barrows—symptoms resemble intoxication: laughter without jokes, grief without names. Coastal routes fear corsairs branded informally as Greywake wolves; inland routes fear press-gangs, toll syndicates, and honest mud.
