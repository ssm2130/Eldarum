Religion checks — what Inye uncovered

Near-legendary Religion rolls — partial truth, maximum danger.

These notes record what the cleric Inye pried loose when dice and vocation aligned—knowledge too obscure for polite pulpits, too structured to dismiss as tavern fiction. Pair them with the Dhotarr dossier and the Cult of Dhotarr article when you need names attached to silence.

This is extremely obscure knowledge—obtained only after rolls of 24–25—and those rolls were still not enough to reveal everything.

Dhotarr is a betrayer god and the god of oppression; in salons he arrives as “The Soul Crusher,” a euphemism elders teach children before they teach them geography. Eldarum once censored betrayer gods’ true names on the theory that starved syllables starve worship—yet contracts still break in his accent.

Dhotarr reached for Anos’s throne during the Purge, that war-in-heaven where portfolios changed hands and losers earned celestial bindings tight enough to echo in Titan strata. That struggle fed the Titan Wars the way embers feed wildfire—same heat, wider stage.

Cults persisted across continents; chronicles describe paladins hunting cells through catacombs and counting houses alike. Texts whisper of the Crushing Depths somewhere in Asceveron as one clandestine archetype—not uniquely tied to a single cult, which makes cartography an act of courage.

An ancient Asceveron sect—the Avatars of Torment—survives mostly as conjecture in foreign tomes, yet regional rumor knots them to Malvolia Sarka, the Iron Maiden. Records feel temporally scrambled against eyewitness testimony the way storm surge scrambles tide tables.
