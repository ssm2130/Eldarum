Scale & mood

Banner photography on the home page hints at the opera; these images stress how weather and geology became divine punctuation. Titans did not “visit” Eldarum — they rented square mileage and paid in altered coastlines.

What is a Titan?

Sages collate here for palace readers and players alike: a Titan is a fully manifested fragment of divine essence — anchored to the Material Plane during the Celestial Alignment, sustained by leakage from outer planes. Titans are not mere servants; they are semi-autonomous, capable of independent strategy, and embodiments of pure domain expression.

Why they walked — Celestial Alignment

While the veil thinned, outer realities bled inward and gods could exert direct influence — yet full divine manifestation would unmake ordinary physics. So powers forged Titans as controlled vessels: continent-sized arguments that could strike without vaporizing the negotiation table.

Titan classification

1. Prime Titans (god-direct avatars)

Mountain-scale combatants channeling a patron directly: godlike intellect, power drawn through a sustained divine link. Their deaths throw off major shards — plot currency your table already hoards.

2. Echo Titans (residual manifestations)

When Primes shattered, energy refused tidy dissipation. It coalesced into unstable remnants — castle- to city-sized, erratic, region-bound. Excellent boss-tier threats and guardians of shard sites.

3. Shardbound Titans (post-war creations)

Mortals meddling with shards — rituals, fusion, ambition — produced artificial Titans: power swings wildly; corruption is common.

Named Prime engines

Verdant Cataclysm (Nila’s Titan)

A living storm of roots, stone, and lightning that could overwrite terrain in heartbeats. Lore in your notes stresses that Nila did not commit a single avatar — she synchronized multiple Titans into one coordinated surge. Scholastic Asceveron argues this cascade ended the open phase of the Wars — and seeded the Scourge.

The Iron Arbiter (Iocetis-aligned)

A construct-like colossus woven from law magic and geometric force. It attempted to stabilize battlefields mid-war; where it succeeded, time sometimes froze in strata — legal absurdities mortals still mine as precedent.

The Chaos Maw (Uccasos-aligned)

An ever-mutating engine that rewrote its own anatomy. Its path broke physics for sport — landscapes liquefied, probability threw temper tantrums. Useful thematic link to Uccasos’s plane in the moral cosmology.

Echo Titans & genius loci

Echo Titans prefigure what the chronicle calls genius loci — spirits of place inheriting stubborn instincts: passes that remember ambushes, kilns that remember prayers. “Neutral” Titans were never harmless; neutrality meant refusing which sponsored catastrophe got the weather budget.

The Scourge — Titan cascade collapse

Your primer names the clash of nature-aligned avatars against rivals as catastrophic — refined here as simultaneous Prime destruction. When Nila’s synchronized strike forced multiple enemies to detonate together, divine energy chained outward: reality fractures, shards, planar scars. This is the in-world excuse for unstable regions Odesus prospectors love.

Entropy & Underworld bleed

Titan deaths vented staggering entropy; scholars tie bleed-through from lower moral planes (Dritora, Zaezirus) to Scar territories. One theory holds that Underworld-adjacent geography formed where Titan death-energy pooled — where rot learned jurisdiction.

For parasitic reflection, green sky, and tendril nervous systems as plane facts, read the Underworld gazetteer.

First Stride & imprints

Before crowns, Titans crossed Eldarum like arguments made flesh — continent-scale hungers whose footsteps wrote valleys and whose idle dreams leaked weather. Mortals later mistook certain ranges for geology when they were posture: mountains that forgot to lie down.

The Veiled Pantheon’s earliest masks were reactions to this scale — gods defined themselves by what they refused to swallow. Storm Lord claimed judgment over sky noise; Forge Twins claimed transformation over raw consumption; Silent Arbiter claimed endings without spectacle.

Gods meet Titans

Divine powers did not annihilate Titans outright — such violence would have unmade the map. Instead gods negotiated angles: binding a Titan’s hunger into a river so it would eat only eastward; trapping another’s curiosity in obsidian lenses so it watched itself instead of cities.

Mirror Sovereign excelled at reflective prisons — showing Titans versions of themselves too honest to endure. Bramble Martyr traded blood for growth, threading painful healing through battlefields so survivors sprouted futures faster than despair.

The Shattering

The war’s named fracture — the Shattering — occurred when rival divine interventions collided mid-continent: chains meant to leash a colossus snapped into glass storms; a Storm spear meant to judge instead ionized an ocean wedge into Odesus’s future deserts.

Titans did not all die. Some shattered into genius loci — spirits of place that inherited stubborn instincts: passes that remember ambushes, kilns that remember prayers, shores that remember fleets that never sailed.

Neutral Titans & genius loci

“Neutral” Titans were never harmless — neutrality at that scale meant refusing to pick which catastrophe to endorse. Some brokered between jealous gods; others slept inside ridges and sent dreams downstream as crop suggestions or plagues — agrarian sorcery mistaken for weather.

Genius loci maintain Titan housekeeping: a genius locus might nurse a battlefield’s shame until fog remembers names, or hum through Zehas tunnels like a conscience made geothermal.

Table hook: stopping “non-neutral” Titans means deciding which inherited chores collapse when the janitor quits — something your wider campaign arc already senses.

Divine factions & proxies

Storm Court heralds mobilized bell networks to synchronize miracles — bells became votes. Forge guilds forged god-marked artillery pieces museums now pretend are art. Silent Arbiter archives swallowed battle names to prevent resurrection-by-memory — some entries still leak as insomnia.

Cults such as Dhotarr’s philosophy of endings echo shard-cults from the Wars — erasures packaged as spirituality for patrons who fear archives more than blades.

Glass wastes & ash thrones

Odesus’s glass deserts fuse sand into blade-edges of memory — prospectors mine Titan heat glass for lenses that reveal ink or guilt. Zehas and neighboring arcs inherit ash thrones — volcanoes as leftover seats where fire aspects once negotiated truces.

Echoes today — War of Gods

Modern crowns fight fleets; theologians fight calendars. Signs interpreted as impending War of Gods often recycle Titan War tactics on smaller scales — miracles stockpiled like ballast, Storm auguries weaponized in budget debates, neutral loci courted by kings who mistake patience for alliance.

Asceveron concentrates these echoes: gods exert leverage through frontier shrines where legal deeds blur; Storm power becomes something adventurers must “ensure” rather than merely worship. The Titan Wars didn’t end — they outsourced their violence to geography and prayer schedules.

Player-facing hook: Investigating divine power in Asceveron is investigating which clause of an ancient armistice still collects interest.
