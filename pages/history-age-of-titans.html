Age of Titans

Mythic epoch when scale broke measurement.

Before crowns, Eldarum knew Titans—entities whose strides carved valleys and whose dreams leaked weather. Mortal theology retrofits them into lineages compatible with modern temples, but frontier sagas preserve messier truths: mountains that stand because a Titan forgot to fall; seas that taste like arguments paused mid-sentence.

Core myths (comparative)

Stammont liturgy speaks of “First Tempest” sowing storm lines Zrelian poets call “braided fire.” Both acknowledge genius loci—localized spirits tethered to battlefields, kilns, sickrooms, or shores—as leftover housekeeping instincts from absent giants.

Material evidence

Expeditions report ossuary strata—bone-like minerals arranged impossibly—below volcanic plugs such as Zehas. Scholars caution against grave-robbing theology; mercenaries caution against budgeting for calm weather.

Comparative mythology

Stammont liturgy calls the first storms “Tempest Sown”—lightning bred as cultivated crop. Zrelian poets speak of “braided fire,” tracing admiralty bloodlines to princes who learned to sail on lava reflections. Lucani archive fragments describe Titans as songs that forgot they required throats. Disagreement is data: each tradition preserves a different failure mode of mortal memory.

Genius loci—housekeeping after giants

When Titans shattered or sank into landscape, attention did not vanish evenly. Genius loci inherit stubborn instincts: passes that remember ambushes, kilns that remember prayers, harbors that remember hulls scuttled for insurance fraud. Neutral loci irritate theologians because they accept offerings from competing temples—fairness mistaken for cynicism.

Shards and moral geology

Prime destruction threw off major shards—plot currency for crowns and criminals alike. Echo Titans patrol ruin perimeters until adventurers volunteer as dessert. Where entropy pooled, Underworld-adjacent scars formed; cartographers draw polite dashed lines; locals carry iron and chalk.
