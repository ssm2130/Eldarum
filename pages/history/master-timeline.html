How Eldarum counts years

Scholars working from Titanic Records and temple copies use PB—ante Bindingum in fashionable universities, “Pre-Binding” in barracks—for the mythic deep past when gods could still manufacture peers without embarrassment. The Purge that bound the worst of the betrayer gods is bracketed roughly 6000–3000 PB in the Common chronicle; precision varies because surviving tablets disagree on whether damnation should be measured in centuries or in verses.

AB (“After Binding”) marks the civic calendars most trade contracts recognize today—Northwake scribes reset tallies after the great compact that ended open divine manufacture of avatars on the Material Plane. AB is not universal: the Cilobrian Republic prefers metric cycles; island chains date from founding storms. When a Collimar charter cites 1559 AB, it is speaking Stammont court time—the same year adventurers might hear farmers call “the ash season that forgot to end.”

Reading advice: Treat every date as a negotiation. Titans did not consult clerks; kings back-filled genealogies; volcanoes veto footnotes.

Mythic prelude

Before crowns, Eldarum’s-story was written in posture: ranges that forgot to kneel, seas that tasted like paused arguments, passes that remembered ambushes before armies did. The genius loci—spirits of place—are not whimsical sprites; they are housekeeping instincts left when divine attention slackened, still fussing over watersheds and kilns centuries later.

Every modern kingdom claims an ancestor who “stood beside” a Titan; nearly all such claims are retrofits paid for in marble. Useful fiction: it buys legitimacy cheaper than armies. Less useful truth: many battlefields still vote.

The Purge centuries

The Common Tome describes a divine tribunal exhausting itself—betrayer gods sealed, followers scattered, Halzartra styled “Fallen One” for classroom propriety. War in heaven leaves paperwork on earth: censored epithets, cults that refuse to starve, and regions where evil aligned populations outweigh tax collectors.

Material outcome: fewer gods walking advertisements for omnipotence, more institutions pretending stability. Mortals interpret the Purge as morality’s victory; cynics note it as market consolidation.

Tribes and the first crowns

By roughly 2500 PB cooperative myths gave way to cooperative logistics—tribes of mixed ancestries sharing belief rather than blood. Metallic and chromatic dragons famously diverged; gem wyrms chose loneliness; humanoids founded federations that would later splinter into kingdoms when grain and bronze allowed ego to outgrow ritual.

The Zrelian Dynasty narrative—god-kings on the southern littoral—sets the stage for theater-of-scale politics: when pharaohs borrow heaven’s vocabulary, farmers learn to parse blasphemy as tax policy.

Titan Wars & Shattering

Divine rivalry reignited when Dhotarr’s chains strained and Ortune answered avatar with avatar. What began as assertion became cascade: gods forged Prime Titans—controlled vessels big enough to argue continents without vaporizing negotiation tables—until Nila’s synchronized nature-host rose to end the open war by ending its instruments.

The Shattering is both metaphor and invoice: simultaneous Prime destruction vented entropy; Echo Titans coalesced as unstable guardians; glass wastes and shard hills became prospectors’ maps. The Scourge, in military histories, names the chained catastrophe where enemy Primes detonated together—reality scarred, morality priced in acreage. Shards, loci & arcane winter

After the wars, arcane practice staggered—libraries went dull, rituals failed politely, scholars blamed “saturation collapse.” Titan deaths pooled entropy; Underworld-adjacent geography formed where moral planes bled upward into stone. Neutral Titans—if neutrality means refusing to pick which catastrophe owns the weather—left genius loci as compromised stewards: unpaid, irritable, occasionally helpful when bribed with ceremony.

Frontier expeditions still quarry shard-strata—some bone-like minerals below volcanic plugs such as Zehas—and call it theology to avoid admitting theft.

Magi-Bane & lost grammars

Later ages imposed divine throttle on how magic re-entered pedagogy. Temples speak of Magi-Bane as benevolent hygiene; archivists whisper about gods removing entire schools of thought from mortal recall so that power could not reconstruct old weapons. Tales from the Asceveron campaign vault describe lost pre-Bane traditions—some resurfacing only after centuries—mirroring the world’s uneven recovery from arcane amnesia.

Whether one counts “689 years of silence” for a given tradition or treats that number as hagiography, the pattern holds: Eldarum’s magic is regulated narrative as much as physics.

Northwake crowns

The Northwake—Asceveron’s theater—crystallized where river kingdoms, admiralties, and march charters overlap. Stammont’s crown lattice stacked ministries into something vertebrae-adjacent; Zrelian seas learned to professionalize prophecy into fleet doctrine; frontier towns like Towfort and Baycliff learned to sell neutrality when crowns blinked.

Collimar grew fat on arbitration: the city eats paperwork and excretes precedent. Charter language from this era still triggers holidays when judges stall.

Three-Crown era

After regency wars nearly let admirals redraw estuaries by fire, the Three-Crown Compact forced Northwake houses into shared succession exams—clerical essays, naval knots, relic challenges—decided under banners of earth, sea, and storm. Lawyers flourished; peasants learned to celebrate procedural delays; adventurers learned that “neutral arbitration” pays in confusion.

Frontier charters (notably the Asceveron march) traded autonomy for levies—tax collectors arrive late, prophets early, mercenaries exactly on time.

Mirror Reach mercantilism

Harbor republics monetized neutrality: bell codes, lantern grammars, insurance cults. The Mirror Reach inner basin rewards pilots who read silver wakes as omens of honest tariffs—superstition profitable enough to codify. Kazon and lesser isles became Greywake clearinghouses where piracy rebrands as privateering whenever treaties blink.

Inland, Greiser roads and Minwall gates throttle trade into theater—smugglers and crown officers share drinks when audiences overlap.

Present tempest

Court calendars near the Asceveron campaign register the mid–1500s AB as a season of sharpened nerves: Zehas exhaling ash into economies that already wheeze; Zrelian hulls testing Stammont assumptions in the Darkshore fog belts; corsairs and chartered companies swapping flags when sponsorship changes.

Storm auguries spike; temples hoard miracles as bargaining chips; scholars who measure morality in watersheds publish alarming maps. Supply columns burn along routes rehearsed in Towfort drills—some blame Black Tusk survivors, others Dhotarr’s whisperers in the baggage trains. For civilian texture—bread riots, charm-bead militias—read Recent strife.

Campaign anchor: Northwake broadsheets and adventuring journals often cite 1559 AB as the “current” civic year—use whichever table your chronicle demands; the tide will not consult your notes.

Historiography—who pays for continuity

Stammont annals emphasize legitimate succession and mineral rights; Zrelian cyclicals emphasize renewal through flame; monastery codices emphasize whichever donor bought ink last month. Truth, in Eldarum, behaves like an average of fears—Titan bones become mining claims; eclipse tables become tax holidays.

Cross-read the Chronicle overview, Age of Titans, and Northwake gazetteer; pack patience, garlic for ink-spites, and coin for archivists who rent keys by the hour.
