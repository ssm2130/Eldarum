Law & Crime

Crowns, charters, and the industries thriving in their shadows.

Stammont law layers royal decree with guild arbitration with temple mediation—trials sometimes resemble concerts where judges conduct tempo. Crime syndicates mimic this structure earnestly; foreign visitors note criminal ledgers stay tidier than municipal ones.

Royal jurisdiction

Treason, taxation, naval impressment—these belong to crowns and admiralties. Advocates trained in cipher law interpret precedents older than living trees; losing parties appeal to storms theatrically if crowds present.

Guild jurisdiction

Mercantile disputes route through syndics who measure penalties in ostracism minutes or subsidized apprenticeships—creative sentencing keeps prisons less crowded than theaters.

Frontier ambiguity

Regions like Asceveron attract bounty charters sometimes contradicting royal letters—adventurers should carry multiple copies of identity and morals adjustable by page.

Notable illicit trades

Curse laundering flows through shell shrines that pretend to be bankrupt monasteries. Pearl smuggling hides inside funeral urn ballast—mourners weep salt; inspectors weep arithmetic. Poets-for-hire draft blackmail sonnets that remain “legal” until the third stanza confesses intent. Ledger recovery brokers move between harbor syndicates and neutral counting houses—those jobs surface verbatim in campaign handouts whenever Collimar’s underworld needs fiction stamped as paperwork.

Which law is “real” here?

Royal statute, Compact arbitration, and temple precedent stack awkwardly. When sources disagree, follow Lore & primary sources: Pantheon manuscripts and plane gazetteers outrank tavern articles; campaign archives outrank fanon for session-specific NPC facts.
